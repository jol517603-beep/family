Sister, forgive me.

a letter folded inside the kerongsang ibu (mother brooch) · winter 1923
Our youngest will not last the night. The midwife came to Eng Neo's door, not to father's. Eng Hooi begs us — "do not let father know, do not let the village know, do not let the child go to a stranger."

I have sworn on the kerongsang. The baby will be raised in the Lee house, by you. We will say the mother went in the night and the midwife brought a foundling. We will never speak our sister's name as the mother.

My husband must not know. The Tans must not know. Only the three brooches will know — Ibu, Anak A, Anak B. Three sisters. One vanished. One raised. One who lied for both.
— Eng Siew

The child sleeps tonight.

a reply folded inside the kerongsang anak A (eldest-daughter brooch) · winter 1923
Sister — I have her. She is small but she breathes. I will name her Lee Beng Guat — bright moon — because she came on the longest night of the year. The Lee surname is hers from this morning.

My husband believes the mother was a goldsmith's helper who went in the night. He has asked me one question and accepted my answer. A Nyonya daughter does not say no, and a Lee husband does not ask why.

One day, when we are all old and the kerongsang have passed to the next neck, someone clever will hold the three brooches together and see the engraving on the back of Anak B — the year a third sister was born and never named again. 1900.

Let them. We did no wrong. We only kept a child alive.
— Eng Neo
