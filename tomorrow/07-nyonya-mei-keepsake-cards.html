林梅蘭
Lim Mei Lan · 1908 – 1996
She was not born a Tan. She married no Tan. No grandchild carried her name.
But for fifty-one years she stood at the back kitchen of the house on Lebuh Aceh,
stirring santan, folding pandan, layering kuih nine times over —
while the family upstairs ate, and laughed, and forgot her name.
Today we remember.
These are her five recipes.

Kuih Talam

兩層糕 · the two-layer cake
green pandan below · white coconut above
Two layers, never one. The green is the earth, the white is the rain. If you rush the bottom, the top will not sit. This is true of cakes, and of people.
serves 9 time 1 h + cool level easy

What you need

Bottom · pandan
  • 120 g rice flour
  • 30 g tapioca flour
  • 30 g green pea flour (hoen kwe)
  • 180 g gula melaka, chopped
  • 250 ml fresh pandan juice (8 leaves blended in 200 ml water, strained)
  • 250 ml water
  • pinch of salt
Top · coconut
  • 50 g rice flour
  • 20 g tapioca flour
  • 10 g hoen kwe flour
  • 250 ml thick coconut milk (santan)
  • 100 ml water
  • ½ tsp salt

How to make it

  1. Bottom layer. Dissolve gula melaka in water on low heat, strain. Combine with pandan juice. Whisk in all bottom flours and salt until smooth. Cook on low, stirring constantly, about 5 minutes until thick like glue.
  2. Steam. Pour into a greased 8" square pan. Steam 20 minutes on medium heat. The surface should look set and matte.
  3. Top layer. Whisk all top ingredients smooth. Cook on low, stirring, about 3 minutes until thickened.
  4. Layer. Pour the white mixture gently over the steamed green layer. Steam another 15 minutes.
  5. Cool & cut. Cool completely, at least 2 hours. Cut into 9 squares with a wet knife between each cut.
Mei's note Cut clean, or don't cut at all. A messy talam tells the cook was angry.

Kuih Kaya

椰漿蛋糕 · coconut egg jam cake
pandan custard above · sticky glutinous rice below
Towkay's first wife asked me for this every Sunday for thirty-one years. She never thanked me. But she never asked anyone else, either. That is also love.
serves 12 time 1½ h level easy

What you need

Glutinous base
  • 300 g glutinous rice, soaked 4 hours
  • 200 ml thin coconut milk
  • ½ tsp salt
  • 2 pandan leaves, knotted
  • 1 tbsp blue pea flower water (optional, for marbling)
Pandan custard
  • 5 large eggs
  • 200 g caster sugar
  • 250 ml thick coconut milk
  • 120 ml pandan juice (6 leaves + 100 ml water)
  • 40 g rice flour
  • 20 g plain flour
  • ¼ tsp salt

How to make it

  1. Base. Drain rice. Mix with coconut milk, salt, and pandan leaves. Drizzle blue pea water in random spots if marbling. Steam in an 8" pan for 25 minutes. Press flat with a wet spoon.
  2. Custard. Whisk eggs and sugar until pale. Add coconut milk and pandan juice. Sift in flours. Strain twice. No lumps allowed.
  3. Layer. Pour the custard gently over the pressed rice base.
  4. Slow steam. Steam on the lowest heat for 40 minutes. Wrap the lid in cloth so no condensation drips down. Toothpick should come out clean.
  5. Cool. Two hours minimum. Then slice with a wet knife.
Mei's note Strain the kaya twice or your guests will know. They will not say it. But they will know.

Kuih Lapis

九層糕 · the nine-layer cake
pink · white · pink · white · nine times over
Nine layers, like the peony on my apron. Nine layers, like the petals of patience. The trick is not the colour — it is the waiting between each pour. Don't rush. The cake remembers.
serves 12 time 1¾ h level easy but slow

What you need

Single batter
  • 250 g rice flour
  • 100 g tapioca flour
  • 50 g hoen kwe flour
  • 250 g caster sugar
  • 500 ml thick coconut milk
  • 500 ml water
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 2 pandan leaves, knotted
To divide
  • Split batter in half
  • Pink half: 4–5 drops red colour (or beetroot juice)
  • White half: leave plain
  • You will pour 9 layers total: pink-white-pink-white-pink-white-pink-white-pink

How to make it

  1. Syrup. Heat coconut milk, water, sugar, salt, and pandan leaves until sugar dissolves. Cool fully. Remove leaves.
  2. Batter. Whisk all flours into the cooled liquid. Strain. Divide in half. Colour one half pink.
  3. Heat the pan. Grease an 8" square pan. Steam empty for 5 minutes on high.
  4. Pour, layer by layer. Pour 1 ladle of pink. Steam 5 minutes on high. Pour 1 ladle of white. Steam 5 minutes. Repeat — alternating — until you have 9 layers total. About 45 minutes of steaming.
  5. Final steam & cool. Steam the last layer 10 minutes. Cool 2 hours. Cut into long strips. Eat with fingers — peel one layer at a time.
Mei's note If the layers blur, your steam was too low. If they crack, too high. Find the middle. Like the middle sister.

Kuih Dadar

椰絲卷餅 · ketayap
pandan crepe wrapped around warm gula melaka coconut
Ah Cai loved this one. He would eat six in a row, standing by the back door. I made it the morning he left for the jungle in 1942 — he took two with him in a banana leaf. He never came back. I still make six every Sunday.
serves 10 time 45 min level easy

What you need

Pandan crepe
  • 120 g plain flour
  • 1 tbsp tapioca flour
  • 1 large egg
  • 180 ml pandan juice (6 leaves + 150 ml water)
  • 120 ml thin coconut milk
  • ¼ tsp salt
  • 1 tsp oil + extra for pan
Inti · coconut filling
  • 200 g freshly grated coconut (white part only)
  • 120 g gula melaka, chopped fine
  • 50 ml water
  • 2 pandan leaves, knotted
  • pinch of salt

How to make it

  1. Make the inti first. Melt gula melaka with water and pandan leaves on low heat until syrupy. Strain. Toss with coconut and salt in a wok over very low heat for 5 minutes — just sticky, not dry. Cool.
  2. Crepe batter. Whisk egg with pandan juice and coconut milk. Add salt and oil. Sift in flours. Whisk smooth. Strain. Rest 15 minutes.
  3. Cook the crepes. Heat a small non-stick pan, lightly oiled. Pour ~2 tbsp batter and swirl thin. Cook 30 seconds — top should look matte. Slide off onto a plate. Do not flip. Repeat.
  4. Roll. Place 1 tbsp inti at one edge of each crepe. Fold the sides in, then roll like a spring roll.
  5. Serve. Warm or room temperature. Best within 4 hours.
Mei's note Don't flip the crepe. One side cooked is enough. The other side should remember it was once batter.

Kuih Bingka Ubi

木薯糕 · baked tapioca cake
deep golden caramelised top · soft chewy middle
The only kuih in this house I baked, not steamed. The towkay's second daughter said it tasted like the sun. I told her the sun was free, and so was this. I lied. The eggs were not free. But the recipe is now.
serves 12 time 1¼ h level easy

What you need

Cake
  • 500 g grated tapioca (fresh or thawed, well drained)
  • 200 g caster sugar
  • 2 large eggs
  • 250 ml thick coconut milk
  • 50 g melted butter
  • ½ tsp salt
  • 1 tsp vanilla (or 2 pandan leaves blended in)
  • 1 tbsp tapioca flour (only if mixture too wet)
Golden glaze
  • 2 tbsp coconut milk
  • 1 tsp sugar
  • pinch of salt

How to make it

  1. Prepare. Pre-heat oven to 175 °C. Grease an 8" square pan.
  2. Drain well. Squeeze grated tapioca thoroughly. Watery tapioca makes a sad bingka.
  3. Mix. Whisk eggs, sugar, and salt until pale. Add coconut milk, melted butter, vanilla. Fold in tapioca and tapioca flour.
  4. First bake. Pour into pan. Bake 30 minutes — top should just be setting.
  5. Glaze and finish. Brush with glaze. Bake another 20–25 minutes until top is deep golden brown and glossy.
  6. Cool fully. At least 1 hour before cutting. The middle firms as it cools.
Mei's note If the top is pale, you took it out too early. A bingka with no colour is a bingka with no story.
