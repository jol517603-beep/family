The Boy in the Storeroom

My great-grandmother slept on a mat in the back storeroom. The household called her just the cook. She had one son, born 1921. The towkay's first wife — the one who wore the brooch Ibu — said the boy was "the cook's child, no relation, no name in the registry." So the boy grew up by the rice sacks, never crossed the chalk line into the front parlour.

But every morning before the towkay walked to his godown, he stopped in the kitchen and pressed one hand on the boy's head. He never spoke. And in 1940, when the boy turned nineteen, he was the one given the brass watch to wind every Sunday morning before tea — the same watch from Fujian. A cook's child does not wind the towkay's watch.
Crack it: Who was the boy's real father? Find the boy and the father on the board, stick them together with a piece of blue tack.

The Third Brooch

Three matching kerongsang sat on the goldsmith's dressing table: Ibu, Anak A, Anak B. One brooch for each of three sisters. The eldest wore Ibu and was buried with it. The middle wore Anak A her whole life. The youngest loved orchids, never married, was supposed to wear Anak B.

In 1923 the youngest "died alone in childbirth" four streets away in a Lee household. The third brooch was wrapped in red silk and locked away forever. The family was told the baby had not survived. The two elder sisters lied for thirty-three years. The baby was a girl. She was raised under the name Lee. She had a daughter. Her daughter had a daughter.
Crack it: Who, on this board tonight, is the youngest sister's blood granddaughter — the rightful heir to the third brooch?

The Watch That Keeps Walking

The brass watch came across the South China Sea in 1891, in the coat pocket of a nineteen-year-old. "Time is the only thing nobody can steal back from you," his father had said. The boy became the founder of See Hai Chan. The watch ticked through three storms, two wives, one war, and a Japanese bomb at the Weld Quay jetty on 11 December 1941. Found three days later in his coat, still ticking.

The first wife took the watch. She gave it only to her own son. He buried it under a kitchen tile through the Japanese years, dug it up in 1946, wore it twenty-two years. On his son's eighteenth birthday in 1968 he laid it on the boy's palm. That son is in this room tonight.
Crack it: Name the three Tan men who carried this watch across four generations — and tell Bibi who is winding it now.

肆

The Cookbook Niece

In 1982 a sixteen-year-old girl from Ipoh walked into the kitchen on Armenian Street with a school exercise book. She had the wrong surname — Lim, not Tan. By every rule of the household she was a stranger. But the old woman at the chopping block looked at her once, at the shape of her face, and silently made room beside her. The girl stood there ten years.

She wrote everything down: the sambal with no recipe, the asam laksa nobody had been allowed to copy. In 1992 she published Resipi Auntie Mei. The dedication: "To Auntie Mei (Lim Mei Lan 林美蘭) — the woman the family forgot to name." First time the cook's full name had appeared in print in seventy years. No Tan blood runs in the girl. None at all. And yet her face is on this board tonight.
Crack it: Who is the girl? What is her exact blood relation to Auntie Mei (one word)? And argue: does she belong on the family tree?
