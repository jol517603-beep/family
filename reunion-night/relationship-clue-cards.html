The Boy in the Storeroom

~ a riddle of two kitchens and one watch ~
My great-grandmother slept on a mat in the back storeroom. The household called her the cook. She had one son, born 1921, in the heat of August, on the kitchen floor. The towkay's first wife — the one who wore the brooch Ibu — said the boy was "just the cook's child, no relation, no name in the registry." And so the boy grew up sleeping beside the rice sacks, eating from a tin plate, never crossing the chalk line into the front parlour.

But every morning before the towkay left for the godown on Beach Street, he walked into the kitchen and pressed one hand briefly on the boy's head. He never spoke. He simply touched, and left. And when the boy turned nineteen in 1940, he was the one given the brass watch to wind every Sunday morning before tea — the same watch that crossed the South China Sea in 1891. A cook's child does not wind the towkay's watch.
Crack it: Who was the boy's father? And what does that make the boy — really — to the family standing in the front parlour? Find the boy on the board, and find his true father, and pin them with a string.

The Third Brooch

~ a riddle of three sisters and one missing baby ~
On the goldsmith's dressing table above the Beach Street shop there were three matching kerongsang: Ibu the mother brooch, Anak A the elder daughter, and Anak B the youngest. One brooch for each of three sisters. The eldest wore Ibu until she was buried with it in 1956. The middle sister, careful and quiet, wore Anak A all her life. The youngest sister loved orchids, never married, and was supposed to wear Anak B.

But in 1923 the youngest sister "died alone in childbirth" four streets away, in a Lee household where no Tan was supposed to know she had been staying. The third brooch was wrapped in red silk, locked in a teakwood box, and never worn again. The family was told the baby had not survived the night. The eldest two sisters lied for thirty-three years. The baby was a girl. She was raised under the surname Lee. She had a daughter of her own. Her daughter had a daughter.
Crack it: Who, on this board tonight, is the youngest sister's blood granddaughter — the one who should have inherited the third brooch? Pin her card to the youngest sister's card with a red string.

The Watch That Keeps Walking

~ a riddle of three men and one ticking heart ~
The brass watch came across the South China Sea in 1891, in the coat pocket of a nineteen-year-old boy who had been told by his father: "Time is the only thing nobody can steal back from you." The boy became a man, became a towkay, became the founder of See Hai Chan. The watch ticked through three storms, two wives, one war, and a Japanese bomb at the Weld Quay jetty on 11 December 1941. It was found three days later in the dead man's coat, still keeping perfect time.

The towkay's first wife took the watch. She did not give it to the cook's boy. She gave it only to her own son. He carried it through the Japanese years buried under a tile in the kitchen floor, dug it up in 1946, and wore it for the next twenty-two years. On his son's eighteenth birthday in 1968 he unstrapped it, wound it twice, and laid it on the boy's palm. That son is in this room tonight, at this table, eating with you.
Crack it: Name the three Tan men who carried this watch across the four generations — and tell Bibi who is winding it now. Find their cards on the board and pin them in a vertical line, oldest to youngest.

The Cookbook Niece

~ a riddle of a wrong surname and a borrowed name ~
In 1982 a sixteen-year-old girl from Ipoh walked into the kitchen on Armenian Street with a school exercise book and a pencil. She had the wrong surname — Lim, not Tan. She had no claim to the front parlour, no claim to the long teakwood table, no claim to any of the three brooches. By every rule of the household she was a stranger. But the old woman at the chopping block looked at her once, at the shape of her face, at the Lim surname, and silently made room beside her. The girl stood there for ten years.

She wrote down everything: the sambal that had no recipe, the asam laksa nobody had been allowed to copy, the exact way to fold a kuih bangkit. In 1992 she published Resipi Auntie Mei. The dedication on the inside cover read: "To Auntie Mei (Lim Mei Lan 林美蘭) — the woman the family forgot to name." It was the first time the old cook's full name had appeared in print anywhere in seventy years. No Tan blood runs in the girl. None at all. And yet her face is on this board tonight.
Crack it: Who is the girl? What is her exact blood relation to the old cook (one word)? And argue with each other for two minutes — does she belong on the family tree? Pin her card wherever your team decides she belongs.
