The story begins in 1898 on Weld Quay. Tan Keo San, a sinkeh from Fujian who arrived at nineteen with one wooden trunk and his father's brass watch, married Yap Eng Siew — eldest daughter of the Beach Street goldsmith house. The Yap girls were three: the old nyonyas of Beach Street called them 三朵金蘭, the Three Golden Orchids. Eng Siew the eldest was the matriarch, the one who would draw a chalk line in her own kitchen in 1920 and mean every inch of it. Yap Eng Neo, the middle, married the quiet Penang Road cloth merchant Lee Poh Soon in 1901. The youngest, Yap Eng Hooi, was born late — eighteen years after Eng Neo — and never married; she died at twenty-three in the back room of the goldsmith house in the winter of 1923, the cause never written down.

Keo San and Eng Siew had three children: Tan Cheng Hoe (1920), the only legitimate son and heir; and the youngest sister Tan Chen Neo (1925). In the back kitchen, the woman from Balik Pulau the family called Auntie Mei bore a fourth child the front of the house never registered: Tan Ah Cai (1925), Cheng Hoe's half-brother, who grew up at Weld Quay among the kulis and walked into the Kulim hills in 1942.

Eng Neo and Lee Poh Soon, in their Penang Road house, raised two children. The elder was their only blood son, Lee Boon Kuan (1918) — never married, died of fever at thirty-two. The younger was their daughter Lee Beng Guat (1921), who walked to school every morning with her brother carrying her satchel.

In 1947, Eng Siew arranged a quiet Penang–Penang alliance: her son Cheng Hoe would marry her sister Eng Neo's daughter Beng Guat. The Tan and Lee houses became one. Cheng Hoe and Beng Guat had two sons: Tan Soon Heng (1948), the elder and gentler, who became a heritage architect and received his grandfather's brass watch on his 21st birthday in 1969; and Albert Tan (1950), the younger, who took an English name in Sydney, modernised See Hai Chan, and married the KL banker Alice Wong in 1994 at St Andrew's, Singapore.

One year later, in November 1948, young Chen Neo at twenty-three became the second wife of the Singapore shipping tycoon Khoo Chin Nam. He died eleven months later. She did not return to Penang. She apprenticed in beadwork on Telok Ayer and stitched for sixty-eight years.

Soon Heng married Lim Soo Hui from Ipoh in 1990 — the girl who had taken the bus to Penang at sixteen with a school exercise book to copy down what Auntie Mei's hands did in the kitchen. They had two children: Bibik Rose (1974), the textile conservator who is hosting tonight; and Dr Tan Wei Wen (1990), the doctor at Penang General. Sixteen years apart.

In November 2020, Wei Wen married Khoo Hooi Wen — great-granddaughter of Khoo Chin Nam through his Singapore first marriage. The wedding closed an old promise: seventy-two years and one week after Chen Neo had married Chin Nam in 1948. Hooi Wen wore Aunt Chen Neo's never-worn peony-toe slippers under her kebaya.

Albert and Alice's only son, Kelvin Tan (1995), lives in London. He runs the European desk of a logistics fund that holds 14% of his own family's company. He takes the See Hai Chan board chair in 2027.

Five families. Twenty people. One stone is the wrong colour.
