The Tan Family

~ stones do not lie ~

陳
TAN · 陳家
How to play: Match each card to its slot on the family-tree board. Read the small "clues" box — it tells you what this person carries, and which other cards they link to. Some cards link to the locked box.

20 cards · 4 generations · 1 reunion night.
1
FOUNDER
Tan Keo San
陳橋山
Founder of See Hai Chan 西海棧 · from Fujian
1872–1941
Stepped off the boat at George Town at 19 with one wooden trunk and a small brass pocket-watch his father had pressed into his palm. Worked the docks as a kuli for seven years — loading, unloading, watching every shipment that crossed the wharf. Saw the gap, borrowed his first lighter, and turned it into See Hai Chan 西海棧 — the family's shipping house. Set the rule that runs the family to this day: "the eldest son carries both the books and the time." Two households under one roof: the front, the back. He drew no chalk line himself — but he agreed to the one his eldest wife drew in 1920.
CARRIES: the brass pocket-watch · See Hai Chan · the rule of eldest carries both · two households under one roof · father of #7 (front) & #9 (back)
2
MATRIARCH
Yap Eng Siew
葉永秀
Eldest daughter · Yap Goldsmiths, Beach Street
1880–1956
Eldest of the three Yap sisters — daughters of the Yap goldsmith house on Beach Street. Her parents agreed the marriage in 1898 because young Tan Keo San had shown real talent and capability at the wharf — and, just as importantly, he had no family in Penang. The eldest Yap daughter would walk into her husband's house as its mistress from day one. She brought a kamcheng of nine gold dumplings and the kerongsang Ibu — the mother brooch — which she wore for fifty-eight years. In 1920 she drew a chalk line across the kitchen floor when the second woman arrived, and meant every inch of it: kitchen behind the line, front rooms in front. She kept the enamel tin on the highest shelf of the bridal cupboard — three brooches inside; only she knew the order.
CARRIES: kerongsang Ibu · the 9-dumpling kamcheng · the keys to the Tan house from day one · the chalk line of 1920 · the enamel tin (placed there by her own hand)
3
KITCHEN
Auntie Mei
美姑
Of the kitchen · name unrecorded
1902–1998
Brought into the Tan house by Tan Keo San in 1920. The eldest mistress allowed her under the roof but drew a chalk line across the kitchen floor on her first morning — kitchen behind, front in front. The chalk line was no joke: it was the agreement between the three of them — the husband, the eldest wife, and her. She would stay. She would cook. She would never cross the line into the front rooms. Her son would not be erased. Never a seat at the tok panjang, never a name spoken in the front rooms. Wore a single pale jade baju kebaya with a permanent sambal stain at the hem. Bore one son to Tan Keo San. For thirty-six years a small envelope arrived for her brother's family in Balik Pulau from the See Hai Chan godown — the ledger called it "oil for the lamp." In her last good year, her own brother's daughter Soo Hui finally published her recipes in her name; a young grandniece named Rose sat beside her on the kitchen stool. She died at ninety-six, three months later — and was carried, for the first time, through the tia.
CARRIES: the stained baju kebaya · 240 unrecorded recipes · the chalk line (from her side) · the monthly envelope from the godown · mother of #9 · aunt of #16
4
SISTER
Yap Eng Neo
葉永娘
Middle Yap sister · wife of Lee Poh Soon
1882–1961
Married into the Lee household in 1901. Wore the kerongsang Anak A from her wedding day. The middle of the Three Golden Orchids 三朵金蘭 — quiet, exacting, the sister you called when something needed quiet. Raised her two Lee children with the same chalk-line discipline her elder sister Eng Siew kept in the Tan kitchen. Her last instruction to the housemaid was: "Put the brooches back in the tin. The owner will come for them when she is ready."
CARRIES: kerongsang Anak A · the chalk-line discipline · the words about the tin · mother (in the family record) of #8 & #12
5
SISTER
Yap Eng Hooi
葉永慧
Youngest Yap sister · died in childbirth
1900–1923
The youngest of the Three Golden Orchids 三朵金蘭 — born late, eighteen years after her middle sister. Quiet, beautiful, fond of orchids. The Beach Street neighbours called her huì-mèi 慧妹. Never married. Died in the winter of 1923 at twenty-three in the back room of the goldsmith house, the cause never written down in any family Bible. Her brooch — kerongsang Anak B — was placed in the enamel tin. Or so the family was told.
CARRIES: the unworn brooch · the orchid name · winter 1923 · a story the family chose not to write
6
LEE
Lee Poh Soon
李寶順
Husband of Eng Neo · the man who asked once
1878–1948
Cloth merchant on Penang Road. Married Yap Eng Neo in 1901. A man of few words and fewer questions. Walked his daughter Beng Guat and his son Boon Kuan to the same school every morning until each turned twelve, regardless of weather. Folded a length of cream silk for Beng Guat's wedding kebaya the year before he died. Never spoke at table about either child's birthday — Lee custom, he said.
CARRIES: the cream silk · the unspoken birthdays · father (in name) of #8 · father (in blood) of #12
7
2ND GEN
Tan Cheng Hoe
陳清和
Only son of Keo San & Eng Siew · 2nd-gen towkay
1920–1995
Raised in his mother's house with the keys jingling at her hip and his father's brass jam saku ticking in the next room. Inherited the watch and See Hai Chan 西海棧 from his father at twenty-one. Married Lee Beng Guat in 1947 — a quiet Penang–Penang alliance arranged by his mother, who said only: "She is the one. Do not ask further." Ran the shipping house for forty-seven years and wound the watch every morning at six. Handed it to his elder son Albert on his 21st birthday in 1969 — the family rule kept. Died at seventy-five in 1995, never knowing that on the same night Albert would push the watch across the table to Soon Heng and keep only the books.
CARRIES: the brass jam saku · See Hai Chan · the marriage his mother arranged · father of #13 & #15
8
2ND GEN
Lee Beng Guat
李明月
Daughter (in name) of the Lees · wife of Cheng Hoe
1921–2005
Born in winter 1921 in the back room of the Beach Street goldsmith house — the family always said her mother had gone there to nurse a sick aunt. Her mother pinned the kerongsang Anak B to her collar on her seventh birthday and told her never to let it leave her: "This is yours. The Lee dressing-table has no other piece to match." Her parents never observed her birthday on a written date — always "sometime mid-winter"; she did not know exactly which day she was born until she was thirty. Married Tan Cheng Hoe in 1947 at twenty-six — wore the cream silk kebaya her father had folded for her the year before. On the morning of the wedding her mother-in-law Eng Siew lifted the Ibu brooch from the tin and pinned it to her collar; her own mother pinned the Anak A beside it; her childhood Anak B already sat at her throat. The kerongsang was complete for the first time in twenty-four years. Took over the See Hai Chan ledgers two years later and ran them for forty-four years. Sent a small monthly envelope from the godown for thirty-six years to a Balik Pulau family — the ledger entry read only "oil for the lamp." On her last night in 2005 she handed her granddaughter Rose the brass key to the bridal cupboard and said: "There is a tin. Open it when you are ready." The tin was marked in her own hand: "Lee — return to Yap if asked."
CARRIES: the enamel tin (3 brooches) · See Hai Chan ledgers · the brass cupboard key · the cream silk kebaya · the unspoken birthday · the words return to Yap if asked · the brooch she has worn since she was seven
9
2ND GEN
Tan Ah Cai
陳亞財
Seoy Yee Kia 細姨仔 · son of Auntie Mei
1925– ?
Born in the Tan kitchen to Lim Mei Lan and Tan Keo San. Never sent to school. Mingled with the kulis at Weld Quay from the time he could walk, and the wharf men called him by the name the front of the house never used: Seoy Yee Kia 細姨仔 — "the mistress's son." When the Japanese came he joined the resistance. After the surrender in 1945 he disappeared into the jungle north of Kulim with the men who would not lay down their arms. The family has waited eighty-one years.
CARRIES: a name no ledger holds · the jungle · Mei's only child · half-brother to #7 (no one ever said it aloud)
10
SINGAPORE
Khoo Chin Nam
邱振南
Singapore shipping tycoon · twice-married
1898–1949
Singapore shipping magnate, head of the Khoo clan's southern arm. Already a widower with a grown son when, in 1948, the families arranged a Penang–Singapore alliance: he married Tan Chen Neo, then twenty-three. Eleven months later, on a humid August morning, his heart stopped at his own breakfast table. The Singapore branch continued through the son of his first marriage — and one great-granddaughter from that line would walk back into the story 72 years later.
LINKS TO: #11 Chen Neo · #19 Hooi Wen (great-granddaughter, first-marriage line)
11
SINGAPORE
Tan Chen Neo
陳珍娘
Singapore widow · beadwork master
1925–2018
Crossed the causeway in 1948 to marry Khoo Chin Nam. Widowed at twenty-four, eleven months later. Refused to come home to Penang. Stayed in her late husband's Singapore house and learned kasut manik beadwork from her stepson's mother-in-law. Became, by her sixties, the named master of three showrooms. Her signature peony-toe pattern hangs today in the Peranakan Museum on Armenian Street. Returned the 1948 wedding slippers to her step-great-granddaughter Hooi Wen in 2018.
CARRIES: kasut manik (peony-toe) · the 1948 slippers · the Singapore door that stayed open
12
LEE
Lee Boon Kuan
李文坤
Son of Lee Poh Soon & Eng Neo · the elder brother
1918–1950
Eldest and only blood son of the Lees. Five years older than the small sister who arrived one morning wrapped in temple cloth — and the only person in the household who never seemed to wonder where she had come from. He simply called her mei mei and walked her to school. Trained as a clerk at his father's cloth shop. Saw his sister married into the Tan house in 1947, then took ill three years later. Died of "a fever that took three days." Thirty-two years old.
CARRIES: the brother who never asked · the empty seat at every Lee dinner since 1950 · brother (in name) of #8
13
3RD GEN
Albert Tan
陳順發
Tan Soon Fatt · elder son · towkay of See Hai Chan
1948–2021
Elder son of Cheng Hoe and Beng Guat. By Keo San's rule — "the eldest son carries both the books and the time" — everything was meant to come to him, and the books did. Took the English name Albert at university in Sydney and never quite put it down. Inherited See Hai Chan at forty-seven when his father died, modernised the warehouses, listed the company on Bursa in 2008. The watch, though, he gave away on the night their father died: he pushed it across the table to his younger brother Soon Heng and said, "You keep the time. I will keep the books." Wore Italian shoes. Spoke Hokkien only to suppliers. The Peranakan side of the family — the kebaya, the kuih, the prayers — passed cleanly past him to his younger brother's house. Died of pancreatic cancer at seventy-three in 2021, leaving See Hai Chan to a board of trustees and his only son Kelvin.
CARRIES: See Hai Chan today (the books) · the modern Western-Baba branch · NOT the watch · NOT the culture · the night he gave the watch away · married #14 · father of #20
14
3RD GEN
Alice Wong
王愛麗
Albert's wife · modern nyonya
1970–today
KL-born, KL-raised, Wharton-educated, Catholic-married. A nyonya by birth — her grandmother kept a brass kamcheng — and a banker by everything she has done since. Married Albert in 1994 at St Andrew's Cathedral, Singapore, in a white gown. Gave their son an English name first and a Chinese name second. Throws Christmas dinner every year, attends Bibik Rose's Tok Panjang every two years out of politeness. Loves the family. Keeps a respectful distance from the kitchen.
CARRIES: the modern Baba-Western fork · the white wedding · the politeness at Tok Panjang · mother of #20
15
3RD GEN
Tan Soon Heng
陳順興
Younger son · architect · Bibi Rose's father
1950–today
Younger son of Cheng Hoe and Beng Guat — two years behind his brother Albert. By Keo San's rule everything should have gone to the elder; but on the night their father died in 1995 Albert pushed the brass watch across the table and said: "You keep the time. I will keep the books." Soon Heng kept it, and has wound it every morning at six since. Restored seventeen Penang shophouses across his career, including the See Hai Chan godown on Beach Street — the building, not the company. Married Lim Soo Hui in 1990 — the apprentice who had stood at Auntie Mei's chopping block. Suffered a stroke in 2024 — the watch now sits on a porcelain dish on his bedside table; his daughter winds it every morning at six in his place.
CARRIES: the brass watch (today, on bedside dish) · the night his elder brother handed it over · the cultural half of the inheritance · seventeen restored shophouses · married #16 · father of #17 & #18
16
3RD GEN
Lim Soo Hui
林素慧
Soon Heng's wife · Auntie Mei's niece
1966–today
Daughter of Lim Mei Lan's only brother. Came to the Tan house at sixteen carrying a small notebook and one question — first to her aunt: "Auntie, may I write down what your hands do?" Auntie Mei looked at her once — at the Lim surname, at the shape of her face — and made room beside the chopping block. For ten years Soo Hui stood there, copying 240 dishes in three-finger and two-thumb measurements; she knew, by then, that the small monthly envelope from the See Hai Chan godown to her father's house was no charity. She did not ask. Published Resipi Auntie Mei in 1992 — dedication: "To Auntie Mei (Lim Mei Lan 林美蘭) — the woman the family forgot to name." Married Soon Heng in 1990. The only daughter-in-law who ever asked, in writing, what had happened to Ah Cai.
CARRIES: the cookbook · 240 transcribed recipes · Auntie Mei's blood (in secret) · the envelope she saw arrive every month · the question about #9 · mother of #17 & #18
17
4TH GEN
Bibik Rose
陳玫瑰
Daughter of Soon Heng & Soo Hui · host of tonight
1974–today
Peranakan textile conservator. The only grandchild of Cheng Hoe & Beng Guat in whom the culture took root — kebaya, kuih, beadwork, prayer. Found her grandmother's enamel tin on the highest shelf of the bridal cupboard in 1998 and did not open it for twenty-six years. When her father had his stroke in 2024 — the brass watch on its porcelain dish, which she now winds every morning at six — she opened the tin, called every cousin still alive, and began compiling this family back into reading. Tonight she has lifted the nine-dumpling kamcheng down from the altar for the first time in seventy years. "Stones do not lie. People forget how to read them."
CARRIES: the master key · the tin (now opened) · winds the brass watch · lifts the 9-dumpling kamcheng · host of tonight
18
4TH GEN
Dr Tan Wei Wen
陳偉文
Doctor · son of Soon Heng & Soo Hui
1990–today
Younger brother to Bibik Rose. Read medicine at NUS, returned to Penang as an internal-medicine specialist at the General Hospital. Met Khoo Hooi Wen at a Singapore heritage conference in 2017. Aunt Chen Neo, then 92, recognised both family names over a Sunday lunch and put the pieces together: "You are the Tan boy. She is my husband's grand-girl. Sit down. I have been waiting seventy years for this dinner." They married in 2020.
CARRIES: the closed-loop marriage of 2020 · the Penang–Singapore door reopened · married #19
19
4TH GEN
Khoo Hooi Wen
邱慧雯
Singapore architect · the bride of 2020
1991–today
Great-granddaughter of Khoo Chin Nam through his Singapore first marriage. Met Aunt Chen Neo for the first time in 2018 — and learned over Sunday lunch that the woman she had been calling aunty-only-by-marriage had been waiting seventy years to hand her something. Wore Chen Neo's 1948 peony-toe slippers under her own wedding kebaya in 2020 — the first time those slippers had been worn since the eleven-month marriage ended.
CARRIES: the 1948 slippers (worn 2020) · closes the Singapore alliance · descendant of #10 (first-marriage line)
20
4TH GEN
Kelvin Tan
陳凱文
Tan Kai Wen · son of Albert & Alice
1995–today
Born in Singapore, schooled in Melbourne, finance in London. Speaks fluent English, conversational Mandarin, and exactly enough Hokkien to greet his father's suppliers. Knows the See Hai Chan balance sheet by heart and could not name three of his great-aunts. Came home for tonight's reunion because his mother insisted — and because his cousin Bibik Rose put a card with his name on it in his hand before he could say no. He is, on paper, his father's heir to See Hai Chan.
CARRIES: the 4th-gen Western branch · See Hai Chan tomorrow · the cousin who came when called
